About the data

What each column in the county table means, and where the number comes from.

The columns

Columns marked Mapped were not available county by county. They were produced by measuring maps against county boundaries. See how the mapping was done below.

ColumnWhat it meansWhere it comes from
County One of West Virginia's 55 counties. U.S. Census Bureau TIGER/Line
Basin Which major river basin the county drains into: the Ohio River or the Chesapeake Bay. This matters because federal watershed funding follows basins. 47 counties drain to the Ohio, 8 to the Chesapeake.Mapped USGS watershed boundaries
Disasters How many times the county was included in a federal major disaster declaration between 2011 and 2024. Extreme heat is not counted, because heat almost never triggers a federal declaration.
Do not add this column up. One storm is declared across many counties at once, so summing the column counts the same event over and over. West Virginia has had 23 federal disaster declarations in total. The most any single county saw is 12 (Lincoln).		 FEMA, via the Atlas of Disaster
FEMA $ Federal recovery and mitigation dollars sent to the county, 2011 to 2024 (FEMA Public Assistance and Hazard Mitigation). This is the county's share only. It does not include HUD disaster grants, which go to the state and are never broken out by county. FEMA, via the Atlas of Disaster
Buyouts How many individual properties have been bought out and cleared because they kept flooding. Funded by FEMA, the Army Corps, the USDA, HUD, or the community itself. McDowell County alone has 708 of the state's 2,826.Mapped WV GIS Technical Center, from FEMA, Army Corps, USDA and HUD records
Rep. loss Repetitive loss areas: places FEMA has formally flagged as flooding over and over. These are areas, not houses. Only 11 of the 55 counties have any. WV Flood Tool (WV GIS Technical Center), from FEMA National Flood Insurance Program records
% in floodplain How much of the county sits inside FEMA's high-risk flood zone, the area with a 1-in-100 chance of flooding in any given year. Statewide, 3.2% of West Virginia is in it. Mason County is the highest at 9.85%.Mapped FEMA flood maps
Flood risk FEMA's rating of the county's overall flood risk, from Very Low to Very High. It combines expected damage, how hard recovery would be for residents, and how well the community can bounce back. The rating is graded on a curve against the rest of the country, so a "Relatively Low" county can still flood badly. FEMA National Risk Index
Natl flood %ile Where the county ranks against every county in the United States for flood loss. 99 means only 1% of U.S. counties lose more. This is the number to trust. FEMA's own "Flood risk" rating above is graded on a curve and buries a small state: it tags seven West Virginia counties "Relatively Low" that sit above the 99th national percentile, including Pleasants and Wirt at 99.9. The median West Virginia county is in the 96th percentile, and 45 of 55 are above the 90th. FEMA National Risk Index
Flood loss/yr FEMA's estimate of how much money the county loses to flooding in an average year, across buildings, crops, and people. This is a projection from a model, not a record of what was actually lost. FEMA National Risk Index
Outage hrs/yr How many hours the average customer spent without power in 2023, on average. Mason County is the worst in the state at more than 14 hours. U.S. Energy Information Administration
SVI A 0-to-1 score for how hard it would be for a community to weather a disaster and recover, based on income, age, disability, housing, transportation, and language. The higher the score, the harder recovery is: 0.90 means it would be tougher here than in 90% of U.S. counties. The CDC calls this the Social Vulnerability Index. CDC Social Vulnerability Index, 2022
% 60+ Share of residents aged 60 or older. 60 is the federal definition of an older adult, under the Older Americans Act. U.S. Census, American Community Survey
Class Whether the county is Urban or Rural. Urban means it contains a city of more than 50,000 people. Most of West Virginia is rural. U.S. Office of Management and Budget, via the U.S. Census Bureau

How the mapping was done

Three of the columns above did not exist as county-by-county numbers anywhere. They came as maps, so they had to be measured against county boundaries to produce a number for each county.

Buyouts. The source is a map of 2,826 individual dots, one per bought-out property. Each dot was matched to the county it falls inside, then counted. Every one of the 2,826 landed inside a West Virginia county, which is a good sign the matching worked.

Percent in floodplain. The flood zone arrives as one continuous shape covering the whole state, with no county lines in it. It was laid over each county's boundary, and the overlapping area was measured and divided by the county's total area.

Basin. Counties were matched to the watersheds they sit in. A county is only counted as belonging to a second basin if a real share of it (at least 1%) is actually there. Without that rule, eight eastern counties looked like they sat in both basins because of tiny slivers of land along the ridgeline, a quirk of the map rather than a real fact. With the rule, only Monroe County genuinely sits in both.

Disaster counts and FEMA dollars match the Atlas of Accountability 2011-2024 workbook exactly, for all 55 counties.
