Where West Virginia actually stands

West Virginia floods worse than almost anywhere in the country. The tools meant to measure that risk are the reason nobody knows it.

The problem

FEMA's own rating makes West Virginia look safer than it is

FEMA scores every county's flood risk relative to the whole United States. In a state where flooding is everywhere, that comparison flattens. Counties that would be alarming anywhere else get graded against a national field and come out looking ordinary.

A label like that does real damage. It is what a grant reviewer sees. It is what a national dataset carries forward. It is how a county with catastrophic flood losses gets sorted into the pile marked not urgent.

The fix is one column. Every page in this project shows the national percentile beside FEMA's rating. When the two disagree, trust the percentile. It is the number that says where a county actually sits against the rest of the country.

The compounding

A flood without power is a different disaster

No pumps. No heat. No medical equipment. No way to call for help. Flooding and outages do not add together, they multiply, and the counties that carry both are the ones where a bad day becomes an emergency the county cannot handle alone.
